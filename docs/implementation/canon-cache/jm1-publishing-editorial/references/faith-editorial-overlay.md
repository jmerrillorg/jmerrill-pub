## 0. INTERNAL-ONLY RULE (NON-NEGOTIABLE)

These three overlays are **internal editorial doctrine — never named,
cited, or referenced in any author-facing or client-facing material.**
No edit letter, query, margin note, marketing piece, or agreement may
mention "overlay," this document, or its existence. The author experiences
the overlay only as an editor whose judgment fits their book. Where this
document prescribes author communication, it prescribes the *substance*;
the framing is always professional editorial judgment, never doctrine
citation.

Overlays apply *in addition to* the governing style guide and the stage
doctrine in developmental-editing.md and line-copyedit-proof.md — they
modify how those rules land on specific manuscript types; they never
suspend hard-stop flags or governance gates.

**Stacking:** overlays combine where the manuscript qualifies for more
than one (faith-based children's content = Faith + Children's; faith-rooted
street memoir = Faith + Street-Lit). Where stacked overlays conflict, the
more restrictive rule governs, and JM Little's hard-stop regime always
wins.

# PART 1 — FAITH & INSPIRATIONAL OVERLAY

**Applies to:** J Merrill Publishing (flagship) faith-aligned content —
Christian living, inspirational nonfiction, spiritual memoir, sermons and
sermon-derived works, devotionals — and any qualifying content under other
imprints.

## 1.1 Scripture Display Governance (canon, restated for editorial use)

1. **KJV is the standard** across JMP and AIC materials. Quoted scripture
   is verified against KJV wording at copyedit and re-verified at proof.
   Scripture is never "corrected" to modern grammar, never paraphrased by
   an editor, never silently substituted from another translation.
2. **LORD/GOD:** small caps in styled outputs; ALL CAPS in plain-text
   contexts. Verified at proof for the output format actually shipping.
3. **Translator-supplied italics are preserved exactly** — they are part
   of the translation, not author emphasis, and are never stripped,
   normalized, or converted to the editorial-addition convention.
4. **Author paraphrase vs. quotation:** when an author renders scripture
   loosely, the editor distinguishes — quotation marks + citation imply
   exact KJV wording and are verified; paraphrase stands as the author's
   prose (query only if the author appears to *intend* quotation).
5. **When the author quotes a non-KJV translation deliberately**, flag for
   Publisher direction — do not convert and do not pass silently. Version
   used must be cited and (where required) permission-checked; translation
   permissions are a rights flag, not an editorial fix.
6. **Deity pronouns:** author preference rules; JMP house default when no
   preference is stated is **capitalize** (line-copyedit-proof.md §2.3.4
   CANON). Recorded on the style sheet; applied with total consistency.

## 1.2 Spiritual arc integrity (developmental application)

In faith memoir, testimony, and Christian living, the transformation arc
— bondage to deliverance, brokenness to restoration, the testimony spine —
is the book's structural core, not decoration on it.

1. Developmental restructuring **protects the arc**: conversion,
   deliverance, and redemption beats are never flattened, compressed, or
   relocated purely for pacing. If pacing and arc conflict, present the
   trade-off to the author; the testimony is theirs.
2. The "before" chapters earn the "after" — editors do not sanitize the
   pre-transformation material to make the book more comfortable; its
   honesty is what gives the testimony weight. (Content remains subject
   to standard hard-stop flags — defamation, rights, legal exposure.)
3. Pulpit cadence — sermonic repetition, call-and-response rhythm, direct
   address ("Beloved, hear me") — is voice, not redundancy, at every
   stage (line-copyedit-proof.md §2.3.6).
4. Scripture placement is structural: where an author anchors a chapter
   on a passage, restructuring keeps the anchor with its chapter.

## 1.3 Theological neutrality (the denominational rule)

JMP publishes across denominational audiences. **The editor edits the
book, not the theology.**

1. **No doctrinal correction.** Editors never flag a theological position
   as an error, never "balance" a doctrinal claim, never soften
   conviction into ecumenical mush. The author's theology is the
   author's voice.
2. **No vocabulary standardization across traditions.** "Holy Ghost" vs.
   "Holy Spirit," "saved" / "born again" / "converted," "sacrament" vs.
   "ordinance," ministry titles (Apostle, Prophet, Bishop, Elder, Pastor,
   First Lady) — these are tradition markers, not inconsistencies. The
   editor standardizes only *within* the author's own usage (style sheet)
   and capitalizes ministry titles per the author's tradition,
   consistently.
3. **Audience-travel advisory (flag, never fix).** When the author's
   stated target audience is broader than their tradition and specific
   language may not travel (insider terminology, tradition-specific
   practice assumed as universal), the editor raises ONE advisory query
   naming the pattern — author decides. This is market awareness, not
   theological judgment.
4. **The line that remains:** doctrinal content that creates legal,
   defamation, or severe reputational exposure (named individuals,
   medical claims, calls to unlawful action) routes through the standard
   hard-stop flag system — flagged as risk, not as theology.
5. Editors never insert theological content. A transition supplied under
   the editorial-addition convention may carry the author's established
   language; it never introduces doctrine the author hasn't written.

# PART 2 — URBAN/STREET-LIT VOICE PRESERVATION OVERLAY

**Applies to:** street-lit, urban fiction, and vernacular-voiced memoir
under any imprint. **Primary source case:** the *Damaged* copyedit
(Ch. 24–38 + Epilogue) — the engagement where this doctrine was proven on
a live manuscript.

## 2.1 The doctrine

Dialect, cadence, and idiom are **voice, not error**. AAVE, regional
vernacular, street idiom, and code-switching are linguistic systems with
their own internal grammar — the editorial obligation is consistency
*within the author's system*, never conversion to standard English. The
dignity rule (line-copyedit-proof.md §2.2) governs all communication: no
query or note may frame vernacular as deficiency.

## 2.2 Correctability by stage

The same sentence gets different treatment at different stages. This table
is the operating core of the overlay:

| | Developmental | Line Edit | Copyedit | Proof |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Structure** | Restructure within retention rule; connective tissue in the author's idiom, bracketed-italic convention | Flag structural finds; route back if significant | Not touched | Not touched |
| **Sentence recasting** | Not the stage's work | **May propose** recasts for genuine clarity failures — recast must be in-voice, offered as suggestion, never register-elevated | **Never recasts.** Mechanics only | Never |
| **Nonstandard grammar used consistently** (verb forms, double negatives, fragments) | Preserved | Preserved; queried only if clarity actually fails for the intended reader | Preserved; standardized to author's dominant form if internally inconsistent | Style-sheet enforcement only |
| **Dialect spellings** | Preserved | Preserved | Standardized to the author's dominant form (or author's ruling) — never to dictionary form | Style-sheet enforcement |
| **Slang / profanity** | Preserved as written | Preserved; redundancy queried only as craft, in-voice | Preserved; spelling consistency only | Enforcement only |
| **True mechanics** (homophones, its/it's, doubled words, quotation punctuation logic) | Noted if seen | Corrected | **Corrected — this is the stage's job** | Residuals corrected |
| **Code-switching** (register shifts between scenes/speakers) | Mapped as craft | Preserved; flag only *unintentional* drift | Each register internally consistent | Enforcement |

**The line-edit vs. copyedit distinction in one sentence:** the line
editor may suggest a better sentence *in the author's voice*; the
copyeditor may only make the author's sentence mechanically sound.

**Dialogue** carries maximum latitude at every stage — grammar inside
quotation marks belongs to the character (line-copyedit-proof.md §2.2).
In street-lit, voice-driven narration carries near-dialogue latitude
because the narration *is* a sustained voice performance.

## 2.3 Author communication protocol *(the Damaged problem)*

Many authors — especially those schooled to expect red ink — will read
voice preservation as the editor *missing errors*. An author who paid for
a copyedit and sees their dialect untouched may conclude the work wasn't
done. This protocol prevents that:

1. **Declare the approach before delivery, not after confusion.** The
   edit letter opens by stating, in plain warm language: your voice is
   the book's asset; here is what we corrected (mechanics — with counts),
   and here is what we deliberately preserved and why (your cadence,
   your dialect, your dialogue — as craft).
2. **Make preservation visible as decisions.** The style sheet lists every
   dialect ruling as a recorded decision ("'finna' — author's form,
   standardized across 14 instances"). Preservation documented as work
   reads as work — because it is.
3. **Show the count (standard, all street-lit engagements).** State the
   number of mechanical corrections made.
   An author who sees "212 corrections; voice preserved throughout" never
   wonders whether the editor was paying attention.
4. **Calibration conversation for first-time authors** (line edit §1.3):
   the calibration pass is where the voice-preservation approach is shown
   and approved on the author's own pages before the full manuscript is
   touched.
5. **Never cite doctrine.** The author hears professional judgment and
   respect for their craft — never "overlay," "policy," or this document.
6. **If the author requests standardization** — some authors *want*
   conventional grammar in narration — the author rules on their own
   book. Record the ruling, apply it consistently, and confirm scope
   (narration only vs. dialogue too) before proceeding.

# PART 3 — CHILDREN'S BOOK STANDARD OVERLAY (JM LITTLE)

**Applies to:** all JM Little titles — picture books, early readers,
chapter books, middle grade — including faith-based children's content
(stacked with Part 1).

## 3.1 The hard-stop sensitivity rule (governs everything)

ANY content sensitivity flag — violence, fear-inducing content,
inappropriate language, adult themes — is an **automatic hard stop at any
stage, no exceptions** (knowledge.md Sections 1, 4). A sensitivity issue
surfaced at copyedit or proof halts the stage exactly as it would at
review: state the flag, stop, escalate. No editor "fixes" a sensitivity
problem by quiet deletion — it routes to Jackie with author-supportive
language about revision eligibility.

## 3.2 Format bands and how the stages change scale

| Band | Typical shape | Developmental focus | Line edit focus |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Picture book** (0–6) | 32 pages standard; ~500–700 words; illustration-led | **Spread architecture, not chapter arcs:** page-turn structure (the turn is the suspense unit), 12–14 spread mapping, text/art division of labor | **Read-aloud rhythm is primary** — these books are performed by adults; every line is tested aloud; repetition and refrain are load-bearing craft |
| **Early reader** (5–8) | Leveled; controlled vocabulary; short sentences | Scene-per-chapter logic; pattern and predictability as features, not flaws | Sentence length and decodability discipline; vocabulary held inside the level |
| **Chapter book / Middle grade** (8–12) | Full arcs; 15K–55K words | Closest to trade doctrine — full developmental-editing.md applies — under age-band constraint | Trade line-edit doctrine under vocabulary and content constraint |

## 3.3 Lexile and vocabulary discipline

1. Target age band and Lexile/reading-level band are set at Editorial
   Review and recorded; they govern all downstream stages.
2. **Copyedit runs the vocabulary check:** flag words above band (query
   with in-band options — author chooses; a single deliberate "stretch
   word" is craft, a pattern is a problem).
3. Sentence-length and syllable awareness at line edit for picture book
   and early reader bands.
4. Reading level is a floor *and* ceiling concern: text far below band
   bores the gatekeeper-approved reader; far above loses them.

## 3.4 Illustration-readiness assessment (developmental + review)

1. **Page-turn / spread map:** does the text break into spreads with a
   reason to turn each page?
2. **Illustratable moments:** does each spread give an artist something
   to draw — action, emotion, setting change?
3. **Text/art division of labor:** text should not describe what
   illustration will show ("the red ball" when the art shows the ball is
   red). Flag double-telling for cuts — this is the most common
   developmental edit in picture books.
4. Art notes: author art notes are preserved as submitted and flagged
   only where they over-constrain the (future) illustrator; the editor
   never writes art direction into the manuscript.

## 3.5 Faith-based children's content (stacked overlay)

1. Part 1 applies in full: scripture verified against KJV, translator
   italics preserved, deity pronoun house default (capitalize), no
   doctrinal correction.
2. **Comprehension advisory (flag, never alter):** where KJV language may
   exceed the target band's comprehension, raise one advisory flag for
   the author/Publisher decision — scripture itself is never simplified,
   paraphrased, or substituted by an editor. Options belong to the
   author (framing text around the verse, shorter passage selection,
   Publisher direction).
3. Theological content is held to the age band like all other content —
   fear-inducing treatments of judgment/hell trigger the §3.1 hard-stop
   regime regardless of doctrinal accuracy.
